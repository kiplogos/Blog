Concrete Cube Test

August 28th, 2023 by Kip Logos

What is a concrete cube test?

The concrete cube test is a common procedure used to assess the compressive strength of concrete. The test involves casting concrete cubes, allowing them to cure, and then subjecting them to a compressive load to determine their strength. The number of days for testing typically refers to the curing period before the cubes are tested for their compressive strength. The standard curing periods for concrete cube tests are usually 7 days and 28 days. These time frames are chosen because they provide a good indication of how the concrete's strength is developing over time.

How to make a concrete cube

It includes:

"Remember that the longer the curing period, the higher the potential compressive strength of the concrete. However, the standard 28-day period is used as a reference point for most construction projects because it provides a reliable estimate of the concrete's strength. It's important to follow established standards and procedures when conducting concrete cube tests to ensure accurate and consistent results. Different countries and organizations might have variations in their guidelines, so be sure to consult the relevant standards for your region."

Materials you will need

The following items should be readily available at your site. Always remember to wear your PPE when working around concrete!

  • Portland cement
  • Sand/Fine Aggregate
  • Coarse aggregates (gravel or crushed stone)
  • Water
  • Mixing tools (wheelbarrow, bucket, or concrete mixer)
  • Mold for casting (usually a cube mold measuring 150mmx150mmx150mm)
  • Vibrating table (optional, for consolidating the concrete)
  • Trowel/float
  • Water curing tank

Safety equipment:

  1. Gloves
  2. Protective eyewear
  3. Mask

Procedure

The following is to be used as a guideline. The specific mix proportions, curing times, and testing methods may vary based on the project's requirements and local standards, so be sure to consult with experts or relevant guidelines in your region.

  1. Mixing the concrete

    2. Measure the quantities of cement, sand, and aggregates based on the desired mix proportions. Common proportions for a basic mix might be 1:2:4 (cement:sand:aggregates). Mix the dry ingredients thoroughly to ensure even distribution. Gradually add water while continuously mixing. Aim for a workable consistency that is not too wet or dry. The mixture should hold its shape without being too soupy.

  2. Casting

    3. Clean and lubricate the inside of the cube mold to prevent sticking. Fill the mold with the prepared concrete mixture in layers. Compact each layer using a trowel or by tapping the mold to remove air pockets. Continue filling and compacting until the mold is completely filled. Overfill the mold slightly and then level off the excess using a straightedge.

  3. Compaction

    4. Hand compaction shall be done with the help of tamping rods so that concrete is thoroughly compacted and completely fills the corners of the mold.

  4. Finishing

    5. Smooth the surface of the concrete using a trowel or float. Allow the concrete to partially set before smoothing the surface again if needed.

  5. Curing

    6. Place the demolded cube in a curing tank filled with water. Keep it submerged for the curing period, which is typically 7 days. This helps the concrete gain strength and durability. Alternatively, you can apply a curing compound to the surface of the cube according to the manufacturer's instructions. This compound helps retain moisture and facilitates proper curing.

  6. Testing

    7. After the curing period, the cube is ready for testing. The main purpose of making a concrete cube is to determine its compressive strength. This can be done using a compression testing machine. Place the cube on the testing machine and gradually apply load until it fails. The maximum load it can withstand is recorded as its compressive strength.

Conclusion

Remember that working with concrete involves safety precautions. Wear appropriate protective gear, and follow the guidelines for mixing, casting, curing, and testing concrete. Additionally, the specific mix proportions, curing times, and testing methods may vary based on the project's requirements and local standards, so be sure to consult with experts or relevant guidelines in your region.

Recycling Construction Materials

October 3, 2020 by Kip

In the ever-evolving world of construction, sustainability has become more than just a buzzword; it's a guiding principle shaping the industry's future. As the global population continues to grow and environmental concerns take center stage, the construction sector is embracing innovative solutions to reduce its environmental footprint. One of the most impactful trends in this journey toward sustainability is the use of recycled materials in construction.

A Sustainable Approach

The construction industry has historically been a resource-intensive sector, consuming vast amounts of raw materials and producing substantial waste. This linear model of take-make-waste is neither economically viable nor environmentally responsible. Recycled materials offer a way to break free from this cycle by reducing the demand for new resources and diverting waste from landfills.

The Many Faces of Recycled Materials

Recycled materials in construction come in various forms, each contributing to a more sustainable building process:

  1. Recycled Concrete Aggregate (RCA):
    2.
    • Crushed concrete from demolished structures can be repurposed as aggregate for new concrete mixes.
    • RCA not only conserves natural resources but also reduces the energy required for concrete production.
  2. Reclaimed Wood
    3.
    • Salvaged wood from old buildings or industrial sites can find new life as flooring, paneling, or structural elements in modern construction.
    • Using reclaimed wood adds character and a sense of history to new projects.
  3. Recycled Plastic
    4.
    • Salvaged wood from old buildings or industrial sites can find new life as flooring, paneling, or structural elements in modern construction.
    • Recycling plastic waste in construction not only mitigates pollution but also reduces the consumption of fossil fuels used to make virgin plastics.
  4. Recycled Steel
    5.
    • Scrap steel can be melted down and reformed into new steel products or structural components.
    • This practice conserves energy and reduces greenhouse gas emissions associated with steel production.

Benefits Abound

In an era defined by environmental consciousness and sustainability, the construction industry is embracing innovative practices to minimize its ecological footprint. One such practice gaining prominence is the utilization of recycled materials in construction. This eco-friendly approach not only conserves natural resources but also reduces waste and energy consumption, making it a win-win solution for both builders and the planet.

The Rise of Recycled Materials

Recycled materials in construction encompass a wide range of resources, from reclaimed wood and salvaged steel to crushed concrete and recycled plastics. These materials are obtained from various sources, including deconstructed buildings, demolished infrastructure, and repurposed industrial waste. As sustainable building practices gain traction, so does the use of recycled materials.

Environmental Benefits

  1. Cost-Efficiency:

    Recycled materials are often more cost-effective than their newly manufactured counterparts. This affordability can benefit builders and clients alike.

  2. Market Demand:

    The increasing demand for sustainable construction practices has created a market for recycled materials. This demand can encourage recycling industries, leading to job creation and economic growth.

Challenges and Considerations

While the use of recycled materials in construction presents numerous benefits, it's not without challenges. Quality control, sourcing reliable suppliers, and ensuring materials meet safety standards are crucial considerations. Additionally, architects and builders must carefully plan and design projects to incorporate recycled materials effectively.

Conclusion

Recycled materials in construction represent a sustainable and responsible approach to building the structures of today and tomorrow. By harnessing the potential of recycled materials, the construction industry can reduce its environmental impact, conserve resources, and contribute to a more sustainable future. As builders, architects, and consumers increasingly prioritize green building practices, recycled materials will continue to play a pivotal role in shaping the future of construction.

Rebar in Kenya: Types and Sizes

October 4th, 2023 by Kip



Reinforced steel bars, often referred to as rebar, are steel rods with surface protruding ribs and straight sections. These ribs and straight portions serve several essential functions in construction, primarily aimed at enhancing the bond between the concrete and the steel bars. Additionally, they provide increased tensile strength, making them well-suited for demanding reinforced concrete projects where structural integrity is paramount.

These reinforcing features, such as the ribs and straight sections, play a crucial role in strengthening the connection between the steel bars and the surrounding cement. This enhanced adhesion capability ensures that the concrete and steel work together effectively, especially when subjected to forces like tension, compression, and shear.

Reinforced steel bars find extensive use in reinforced concrete projects that demand exceptional strength. These projects include high-rise buildings, bridges, dams, and other infrastructure where structural stability and load-bearing capacity are critical considerations. The utilization of rebar not only reinforces the concrete but also contributes to the longevity and durability of the structure.

There are different types of deformed steel bars in use in Kenya. The sizes include D8, D10, D12, D16, D20, D25, and D32. The D stands for the diameter of the steel.

In summary, reinforced steel bars, with their ribbed and straight surface features, play a vital role in enhancing the bond between concrete and steel, resulting in robust tensile strength. This makes them a suitable choice for reinforced concrete projects that require exceptional structural integrity and load-bearing capacity.
